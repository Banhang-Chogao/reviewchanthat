I have been testing handheld gaming PCs since the original Steam Deck landed in 2022. I have owned an Asus ROG Ally, an Ally X, a Legion Go, and I have spent enough hours with the original MSI Claw to know exactly where Intel's first attempt fell short. When MSI announced the Claw 8 EX AI+ with the new Intel Arc G3 Extreme processor—a chip built from the ground up for handhelds—I was skeptical. The original Claw was a disappointment. The Arc G3 Extreme, however, is not a refresh; it is a complete rethinking of what a mobile gaming GPU can do at 30 watts.

I have been using the Claw 8 EX AI+ as my primary gaming device for ten days. I have played through entire sections of Cyberpunk 2077, DOOM: The Dark Ages, Indiana Jones and the Great Circle, and Baldur's Gate 3. I have run it on battery, plugged in, in bed, on a train, and connected to a 4K monitor. This is my honest, unfiltered take.

## What Has Changed? A Lot More Than the Name

The original MSI Claw used Intel's Meteor Lake chips with integrated Arc graphics. The problem was never raw compute; it was drivers, power management, and the fact that those chips were never designed for a handheld form factor. The Claw 8 EX AI+ uses the Intel Arc G3 Extreme, a processor that shares architectural DNA with Intel's discrete Arc B-series GPUs but is optimised specifically for low-power, high-efficiency scenarios.

The first thing you notice is the chassis. MSI has listened to every complaint about the original. The grips are thicker, the weight distribution is better, and the 8-inch 120Hz IPS display with full VRR support is genuinely beautiful. At 500 nits peak brightness, it is usable outdoors in the shade. The 1920x1200 resolution is the sweet spot for a screen this size — sharp enough for text, but not so demanding that games struggle to hit 60 fps.

The new Cooler Boost HyperFlow system is whisper-quiet during light use and only becomes audible under sustained AAA load, and even then it is more of a low whoosh than a high-pitched whine. I measured surface temperature after an hour of Cyberpunk 2077 at medium settings: the centre of the screen was 38°C, the grips stayed below 32°C, and the hottest point on the back was a tolerable 42°C. That is significantly better than the Steam Deck OLED, which can hit 45°C on the backplate under similar load.

## That Display Deserves a Paragraph of Its Own

I need to spend a moment on the screen because it is the best I have seen on a handheld gaming PC. The 8-inch IPS panel covers 100 percent of sRGB, and even in HDR content — yes, it supports HDR — it hits a measured peak of 480 nits in real-world usage. The 120Hz refresh rate with full variable refresh rate means that whether you are getting 40 fps or 90 fps, the image stays buttery smooth without tearing.

I played DOOM: The Dark Ages with VRR enabled, and even when the frame rate dipped into the high 40s during chaotic battles with multiple demons and particle effects, the experience remained fluid. On the Steam Deck LCD, those same dips feel like stuttering. On the Claw 8, they are barely noticeable. If you have been holding out for a handheld with a truly great display, this is it.

## Intel Arc G3 Extreme: The Real Story

The Arc G3 Extreme is built on Intel's Xe3 architecture with 12 Xe cores. That may not sound like much compared to a desktop GPU with 80 or 100 compute units, but context matters. This chip operates between 15 and 35 watts. At 30 watts, the Arc G3 Extreme delivers performance that rivals the Z1 Extreme in the ROG Ally X in rasterisation — and outright beats it in ray tracing and AI upscaling.

XeSS 3 with 4x multi-frame generation is the headline feature. In practice, it transforms unplayable scenarios into smooth experiences. In Cyberpunk 2077 with ray tracing set to medium and XeSS set to performance with frame generation enabled, I averaged 62 fps through the crowded market scene — a scene that hovers around 28 fps on the ROG Ally X with FSR 2 set to performance. The difference is night and day.

Here are my benchmark numbers, all taken at 1200p on the device itself:

**Cyberpunk 2077 (medium, XeSS Performance + FG):** 62 fps average, 48 fps 1% low.
**Cyberpunk 2077 (medium, no upscaling):** 38 fps average, 29 fps 1% low.
**DOOM: The Dark Ages (high, dynamic resolution):** 75 fps average, 58 fps 1% low.
**Indiana Jones and the Great Circle (medium, XeSS Quality):** 52 fps average, 41 fps 1% low.
**Baldur's Gate 3 (medium, FSR 2 Quality):** 45 fps average in Act 3, 34 fps 1% low.
**Shadow of the Tomb Raider (highest, no upscaling):** 58 fps average.

These numbers tell a clear story: the Arc G3 Extreme can handle any current-generation AAA title at 1200p with medium settings and upscaling. For esports titles like Valorant, Fortnite, and Overwatch 2, you can crank settings to high and easily hit 90-120 fps.

## Battery Life: The Endurance Mode Changes Everything

I wanted to test battery life in three scenarios: light indie gaming, AAA gaming with default settings, and AAA gaming with Intel's Endurance Gaming mode enabled.

With the 53 Wh battery (up from 43 Wh in the original Claw), I played Hades II for three hours and 45 minutes before the device shut down — screen at 50 percent brightness, 60Hz mode, TDP set to 15 watts via the MSI Center. That is competitive with the ROG Ally X and significantly better than the original Claw.

For AAA gaming, I ran Cyberpunk 2077 at medium settings with XeSS Performance and frame generation. The device lasted one hour and 58 minutes. That is about 20 minutes longer than the ROG Ally X under the same test, and about 40 minutes longer than the Steam Deck OLED.

Endurance Gaming mode is the hidden gem. It caps TDP at around 15 watts, disables frame generation, and optimises voltage curves. With Endurance mode enabled, Cyberpunk 2077 ran at a locked 30 fps (which is perfectly playable for a handheld) and the battery stretched to three hours and 12 minutes. That is enough for a long flight from Ho Chi Minh City to Tokyo without needing to hunt for an outlet.

## The Software Experience: MSI Center M Has Grown Up

MSI Center M, the front-end launcher, has received a complete overhaul since the original Claw launched. It now feels like a legitimate alternative to SteamOS rather than a clunky afterthought. Game library integration with Steam, Xbox Game Pass, Epic Games Store, GOG, and Ubisoft Connect works seamlessly. The quick settings panel gives you granular control over TDP, fan curves, resolution, and refresh rate without needing to dive into submenus.

There is still some bloatware. MSI preloads a handful of sponsored games and toolbars that you will want to uninstall immediately. And Windows 11 on an 8-inch screen remains imperfect — there are times when you will wish for SteamOS's big-picture simplicity. But with handheld-friendly tools like Playnite and Handheld Companion, you can build an experience that rivals the Deck.

## Where It Stumbles

Nothing is perfect, and the Claw 8 EX AI+ has real flaws. The first is price: at $1,799 for the configuration I tested (32GB RAM, 1TB SSD), it is the most expensive handheld gaming PC on the market. You can buy a Steam Deck OLED and a ROG Ally X for the same money. Is the performance uplift worth it? For enthusiasts, yes. For casual gamers, probably not.

The second issue is the speakers. They are adequate for gaming — dialogue is clear, explosions have some punch — but they lack bass and get muddy at high volume. If you care about audio quality, you will want to use wired earbuds or something like the Nothing Ear (2).

Third, the SSD is technically user-swappable, but MSI has hidden it behind a tricky internal bracket. Replacing the drive requires removing the heatshield and carefully detaching a ribbon cable. It is doable with a YouTube guide and the right screwdriver, but it is not as user-friendly as the Steam Deck's tool-free SSD bay.

Fourth, Windows Updates. I lost an evening to a Windows Update that decided to install itself during a gaming session, causing the device to reboot and corrupt my save progress in Indiana Jones. This is not MSI's fault — it is a Windows-on-handheld problem — but it is a real annoyance. The workaround is to set active hours in Windows Update settings, but it should not require workarounds.

## Who Should Buy This?

The MSI Claw 8 EX AI+ is not a mainstream device. It is expensive, it runs Windows (with all the baggage that entails), and it is heavier than a Steam Deck. But if you are the kind of person who reads GPU benchmarks for fun, who wants to play Cyberpunk 2077 with ray tracing on a train, who values VRR and a 120Hz screen over battery life, this is the best handheld gaming PC money can buy right now.

For everyone else, the Steam Deck OLED at $549 remains the smarter choice. It is cheaper, has a better software experience, and is good enough for most people. The Claw 8 EX AI+ is for the enthusiasts who want the absolute best performance at any cost.

## Docked Mode and Desktop Performance

I spent an afternoon with the Claw 8 EX AI+ connected to a 27-inch 4K monitor via USB-C, paired with a Bluetooth keyboard and mouse. The Arc G3 Extreme handles desktop productivity effortlessly — 4K YouTube at 60 fps, light photo editing in Affinity Photo, even some DaVinci Resolve timeline scrubbing at 4K. Gaming at 4K is not realistic for AAA titles, but at 1440p with XeSS Quality, I played Hades II at a solid 90 fps and Forza Horizon 5 at 55 fps with high settings.

The device supports Thunderbolt 4 over both USB-C ports, which means you can connect an eGPU if you want desktop-class gaming performance when docked. I tested it with an Intel Arc A770 external GPU, and the setup worked without driver conflicts — a welcome surprise given how finicky eGPU support has historically been on Windows handhelds. With the A770 connected, Cyberpunk 2077 ran at 1440p high settings at a locked 75 fps. That flexibility turns the Claw 8 from a pure handheld into a genuine desktop replacement if you invest in the ecosystem.

The one missing feature is USB4 with 40Gbps on both ports. Only the left USB-C port supports the full 40Gbps bandwidth; the right port is limited to 10Gbps. This is clearly documented in the spec sheet, but it is easy to miss, and it means you need to pay attention to which port you use for a display or eGPU connection.

## How It Compares to Every Major Handheld

I have owned or extensively tested every major handheld gaming PC on the market, so here is how the Claw 8 EX AI+ stacks up in practical terms:

**Steam Deck OLED (2024):** The Deck is lighter, cheaper, and has a vastly better software experience with SteamOS. But the Claw is roughly 80 percent faster in GPU-bound scenarios, has a higher-resolution display with VRR, and can play titles like Starfield and Alan Wake 2 that struggle on the Deck. The Deck wins on battery life for indie games (six to eight hours versus four to five on the Claw). For AAA games, they are surprisingly close — the Claw pulls ahead by about 30 minutes thanks to Endurance mode.

**Asus ROG Ally X (2024):** The Ally X was the benchmark before the Claw 8 EX AI+. In raw rasterisation performance, they are close — the Z1 Extreme still holds its own. But the Claw pulls ahead significantly in ray tracing workloads (roughly 2x better performance with RT enabled) and in upscaling quality. XeSS 3 produces noticeably cleaner images than FSR 2 at the same quality preset. The Ally X has a better speaker system and a more mature software ecosystem, but the Claw has the better screen and better cooling.

**Lenovo Legion Go (2024):** The Legion Go has a larger 8.8-inch screen and detachable controllers, but its Z1 Extreme chip is outperformed by the Arc G3 Extreme by a significant margin. The Legion Go also lacks VRR, which makes frame rate dips more noticeable. If you want the biggest screen, go with the Legion Go. If you want the best gaming performance, the Claw is the clear winner.

**Original MSI Claw (2024):** There is no comparison. The original Claw was a beta product — drivers were buggy, performance was inconsistent, and battery life was poor. The Claw 8 EX AI+ addresses every single criticism. If you owned the original and were burned by it, I understand your skepticism. But this is a completely different machine in every way that matters.

## The XeSS 3 Deep Dive

XeSS 3 with 4x multi-frame generation is the defining feature of the Arc G3 Extreme, and it deserves a detailed breakdown because it is the primary reason this chip can deliver AAA performance at 30 watts.

Traditional upscaling renders the game at a lower internal resolution and reconstructs it to your target resolution. Frame generation goes further by interpolating entire frames between rendered ones, effectively doubling or quadrupling the perceived frame rate. XeSS 3 does both in a single pipeline, and it does so with dedicated hardware on the chip — the 12 Xe cores include XMX (Xe Matrix eXtension) units that handle the AI workload without consuming CPU cycles.

In practice, this means that in titles with XeSS 3 support, you can run the game at 720p internal resolution with medium settings and get a smooth 60 fps experience that looks nearly as good as native 1200p. The motion fluidity is excellent — I noticed very few artefacts even during fast camera movements in DOOM: The Dark Ages. The only scenario where artefacts become visible is in UI elements and text during movement, which is a limitation shared with DLSS 3 and FSR 3.

The bad news is that XeSS 3 support is not universal. Currently, about 80 games support XeSS, and only a subset of those support XeSS 3's frame generation. Intel has been aggressive with developer outreach, and most major 2025 and 2026 releases include XeSS 3 at launch, but older titles will need to rely on standard XeSS or FSR 2, where the performance advantage over the Z1 Extreme narrows considerably. For older games without upscaling support, the raw 30-watt rasterisation performance is comparable to the Ally X — impressive, but not generationally ahead.

## Real-World Noise and Heat

I measured fan noise with a decibel meter at a distance of 30 centimetres — roughly the distance from the device to your ears during normal use. The results varied significantly based on workload and power mode:

Balanced mode (default, 25W TDP): 32 dB under light load (menu navigation, web browsing), 38 dB during indie gaming, 45 dB under sustained AAA load. This is quiet enough that you will not notice it with headphones on and barely noticeable in a quiet room without audio.

Performance mode (30W TDP): 35 dB idle, 48 dB under AAA load. The fans are audible but not intrusive. The pitch is low and even — no whining or rattling.

Endurance mode (15W TDP): 28 dB idle, 34 dB under load. Virtually silent. This is the mode to use on flights or in bed while your partner is sleeping.

For comparison, the Steam Deck OLED measures 42 dB under AAA load, and the ROG Ally X hits 44 dB. The Claw 8 EX AI+ is measurably quieter than both, which is impressive given that it is dissipating more heat (30W versus the Deck's 15W and the Ally X's 25W). The Cooler Boost HyperFlow system deserves the credit — the positive pressure design and larger fan blades move more air at lower RPM.

After ten days with this device, I am keeping it. I have not felt this excited about a handheld since the original Steam Deck. Intel and MSI have built something genuinely special. It is not perfect, but it is the first handheld that makes me believe the PC handheld form factor can deliver a true AAA experience without compromise.

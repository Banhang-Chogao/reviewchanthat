## The Phone I Did Not Want to Like

I will start with a confession. When Samsung announced the Galaxy S26 Ultra in January 2026, I was skeptical. The S25 Ultra was already a refinement of the S24 Ultra, and the S24 Ultra was itself a refinement of the S23 Ultra. Three generations of iterative improvement had turned the Ultra series into a predictable annual cycle: better titanium finish, slightly improved cameras, faster chipset, higher price. I expected more of the same.

Six months later, I have to admit I was wrong.

Not completely wrong — the S26 Ultra is not a revolutionary device. It does not reinvent the smartphone. But it is the most complete Android phone I have ever used, and after half a year of daily use, I have developed a relationship with it that I did not expect. It is the phone I reach for when I need to get work done, the phone I trust for photography, and the phone that has quietly replaced my tablet for most tasks.

This review is not a spec sheet. It is a six-month diary of living with a device that has become, against my initial judgment, genuinely indispensable.

## Design and Build: The Refinement You Cannot See in Photos

The first thing you notice about the S26 Ultra is that it feels different from the S25 Ultra, even though they look almost identical in renders. Samsung refined the frame geometry — the corners are slightly more rounded, the titanium frame has a softer matte finish, and the weight distribution is marginally better. These are small changes that do not show up on a spec sheet but make a real difference when you hold the phone for hours.

The S26 Ultra is still a large phone. At 234 grams with a 6.9-inch display, it is not a one-handed device unless you have unusually large hands. But the weight is distributed evenly, and the slightly curved back panel helps it sit more naturally in the palm. After six months, I no longer notice the size unless I pick up a smaller phone and realize how much screen real estate I have gotten used to.

The titanium frame has held up well. After six months of use without a case — I tested it both with and without — the frame shows minor micro-abrasions around the charging port and the bottom corners, but nothing that is visible from normal viewing distance. The Gorilla Glass Victus 3 front and back are scratch-free, which is surprising given that I have dropped the phone twice onto tile floors (both times with a thin case on).

The integrated S Pen is the most underrated design decision Samsung made. Unlike the S25 Ultra which required a separate silo, the S26 Ultra's S Pen sits flush inside the device and can be ejected with a satisfying click. I use it far more than I expected — for handwriting quick notes in meetings, for precise photo editing, and for signing documents. It is one of those features you do not think you need until you have it, and then you cannot imagine going back.

## Display: The Best Screen I Have Used

The 6.9-inch Dynamic AMOLED 3X display is, without qualification, the best screen I have ever used on a phone. Samsung increased the peak brightness to 3,200 nits, which sounds like a marketing number, but I have tested it under direct sunlight in July in Vietnam — the screen is readable, even with sunglasses on.

The 1-144Hz variable refresh rate is buttery smooth. Scrolling through long articles, social media feeds, and documents feels fluid in a way that 120Hz screens no longer do. The difference is subtle but addictive — once you get used to 144Hz, 60Hz feels like a slideshow.

But the real improvement is in color accuracy and viewing angles. Samsung has historically oversaturated its displays to make them pop in store comparisons. The S26 Ultra finally offers a natural color mode that does not sacrifice vibrancy. Skin tones look realistic. Whites are neutral. And HDR content — especially on YouTube and Netflix — looks genuinely cinematic.

I have compared the display side by side with the iPhone 17 Pro Max. The iPhone has slightly better out-of-the-box color accuracy, but the Samsung has higher peak brightness and smoother refresh rate. It comes down to personal preference, but for media consumption, the S26 Ultra is the winner.

## Camera: Consistency Over Hype

After six months, I have taken roughly 4,000 photos with the S26 Ultra. That is not a flex — it is simply what happens when you carry a good camera everywhere. I have used it for travel photography in Da Nang and Phu Quoc, for food photos at home, for portraits of friends, and for documentation at work.

The camera hardware is familiar on paper: a 200MP main sensor, a 12MP ultrawide, a 10MP 3x telephoto, and a 50MP 5x periscope telephoto. The front camera is 12MP. These numbers are almost identical to the S25 Ultra. The difference is in processing.

Samsung finally fixed its shutter lag problem. The S25 Ultra had a noticeable delay between pressing the shutter and capturing the image, which made it miss candid moments. The S26 Ultra is instantaneous. I can capture a moving child, a jumping dog, or a passing motorbike without blur. This alone is worth the upgrade if you take photos of people or pets.

The 200MP mode is genuinely useful now. On the S23 Ultra and S24 Ultra, 200MP mode produced files that were too large and too noisy to be practical. The S26 Ultra's processing pipeline handles high-resolution captures better — the files are still large (around 30-40MB each), but the detail is real. I have used 200MP mode for landscape photos that I later cropped aggressively, and the results are usable at print sizes up to A2.

The telephoto cameras are where Samsung has pulled ahead of the competition. The 5x optical zoom at 50MP produces sharp, detailed images even in moderate light. The 10x hybrid zoom is surprisingly good — I have taken photos of street signs and menu boards from across a restaurant that are legible. The 100x Space Zoom is still a gimmick, but a fun one. I have used it exactly three times in six months: once to read a faraway sign, once to look at the moon, and once to creep on my neighbor's cat (the cat was fine).

Low-light performance is excellent but not best-in-class. The Pixel 11 still edges out the S26 Ultra in very dark conditions, with cleaner shadows and more natural color reproduction. Samsung's night mode tends to over-brighten scenes, making them look artificial. But for 90 percent of low-light situations — dinner at a restaurant, street photography at dusk, indoor events — the S26 Ultra produces images that are good enough to share without editing.

Video recording has improved significantly. The 8K mode at 30fps is now usable for short clips, and the stabilization at 4K 60fps is impressive. I shot handheld video while walking through Ben Thanh Market, and the result was stable enough to use without a gimbal. The audio recording has also improved — the directional audio capture does a reasonable job of isolating the subject's voice in noisy environments.

## Battery and Charging: The Unsung Hero

The S26 Ultra has a 5,500mAh battery, which is large but not the largest on the market. What matters is how Samsung optimized the power management with the Snapdragon 8 Gen 5 chipset and One UI 9.

After six months, my typical day looks like this: I wake up at 7am with 100 percent battery. I use the phone throughout the day for messaging, email, social media, GPS navigation (about 30 minutes), camera (about 20-30 photos), and YouTube (about an hour). By 10pm, I usually have between 20 and 30 percent remaining. On heavier days — extended camera use, video calls, or gaming — I end the day around 10-15 percent.

This is with the display set to QHD+ resolution and adaptive refresh rate. If I drop to FHD+, I can add another hour or two of screen time. Charging is adequate at 45W wired and 15W wireless. The 45W charging takes about an hour to go from zero to full. This is slower than Chinese competitors (Xiaomi offers 120W, OnePlus offers 100W), but it is fast enough for overnight charging or a top-up during lunch.

Wireless charging is convenient but slow. I use a Samsung wireless charger on my desk, and it adds about 30 percent per hour. It is good for maintaining battery during the workday but not for emergency charging.

The battery health after six months shows minimal degradation. AccuBattery reports my battery capacity at approximately 96 percent of the original, which is excellent. Samsung's battery protection features — limiting charge to 85 percent for overnight charging — help extend long-term battery life.

## One UI 9: The Software That Grew on Me

I have never been a fan of Samsung's software. One UI has always felt like a heavy skin on top of Android, with duplicated apps, confusing settings, and a general lack of polish compared to stock Android or even Xiaomi's HyperOS. One UI 9 changed my mind.

The interface is cleaner and more intuitive. Samsung finally stopped pushing its own apps over Google's — you can disable or uninstall most Samsung apps, and the setup process does not force you to create a Samsung account. The notification shade is streamlined, the quick settings are customizable, and the overall aesthetic is closer to stock Android than any previous One UI version.

The new AI features are genuinely useful, not just marketing bullet points. Here is what I actually use:

**AI Select:** This replaces the old Edge Panel. You circle or highlight content on screen — text, images, or videos — and the AI suggests actions. I use it most for extracting text from screenshots and for quickly saving images without downloading them.

**Photo Assist:** The generative edit tools work well. I have removed tourists from travel photos, expanded backgrounds, and adjusted lighting. The results are good enough for social media. For professional photography, you still want Lightroom, but for quick edits on the go, Photo Assist is impressive.

**Live Translate:** I use this mostly for reading Vietnamese signs and menus translated into English. The real-time translation in the camera viewfinder works reasonably well for simple text. For complex sentences, it struggles with grammar, but it is better than nothing.

**Browsing Assist:** This summarizes long articles. I use it for work — it saves me time reading through lengthy reports and documents. The summaries are accurate about 80 percent of the time, and I have learned to spot when the AI misses nuance.

The AI features run on-device using the NPU in the Snapdragon 8 Gen 5. Most operations are instant or near-instant. Cloud-dependent features — like generative photo edits — take a few seconds but are fast enough for casual use.

One UI 9 also improves multitasking. The S26 Ultra supports up to four apps in split-screen with a floating window. I regularly use two apps side by side — notes and a browser, or maps and messaging — and the experience is smooth. The large display makes split-screen genuinely usable in a way that smaller phones cannot match.

Samsung's update commitment is also worth mentioning. The S26 Ultra promises seven years of OS upgrades and security patches. Samsung has been consistent with monthly security updates — I have received every update on time, and One UI 9.1 arrived in June 2026 with meaningful improvements.

## Performance: More Than Enough

The Snapdragon 8 Gen 5 is fast. Really fast. Apps open instantly, multitasking is fluid, and even demanding games run at maximum settings without thermal throttling.

But here is the honest truth: the performance difference between the S26 Ultra and the S25 Ultra is barely noticeable in daily use. The S25 Ultra was already fast enough for every task most people throw at a phone. The S26 Ultra's chipset advantages show up in AI processing — image generation, real-time translation, and photo editing are noticeably faster — and in sustained performance during extended gaming sessions.

Gaming performance is excellent. I play Genshin Impact and Honkai Star Rail at max settings, and both run at a stable 60fps with no stuttering. The phone gets warm — not hot — after extended sessions, but it never throttles down to the point of affecting gameplay.

The 12GB of RAM is sufficient for my workflow. I rarely notice apps refreshing, even with 20+ tabs open in Samsung Internet and a dozen apps in the background. The 256GB base storage is adequate, but I opted for the 512GB version and have used about 200GB after six months — mostly photos, videos, and offline maps.

## What I Do Not Like

No review is honest without complaints. Here are mine.

**The camera bump is too large.** The S26 Ultra does not lie flat on a table. When you place it on a desk and tap the top-left corner of the screen, the phone wobbles. Every. Single. Time. This is a minor annoyance, but it speaks to a design compromise that Samsung has not solved.

**Fingerprint sensor position is awkward.** The ultrasonic in-display fingerprint sensor works well — it is fast and accurate — but the position is too low. My thumb naturally rests higher on the screen. After six months, I still occasionally miss the sensor on the first attempt.

**Charging speed is behind the competition.** 45W is fine, but when OnePlus charges at 100W and Xiaomi at 120W, Samsung's charging feels slow. The S26 Ultra takes almost twice as long to charge as a OnePlus 13.

**AI features are gated behind Samsung account.** Several useful AI features require a Samsung account and, in some cases, a Samsung Cloud subscription. This adds friction. I understand the business rationale, but it feels nickel-and-dimey for a $1,300 phone.

**The phone is slippery without a case.** The matte titanium frame and glass back look premium but offer little grip. I have almost dropped the phone several times when using it one-handed. A case is essential, which is a shame because the phone looks great without one.

## The Six-Month Verdict

After six months, here is where I stand.

The Samsung Galaxy S26 Ultra is the best Android phone you can buy in July 2026. It is not the most innovative — the Samsung Tri Fold wins that award. It is not the best value — the OnePlus 13 offers 90 percent of the experience at 60 percent of the price. But it is the most complete, most refined, and most reliable Android flagship on the market.

The camera system is versatile enough for any situation. The battery lasts all day. The display is stunning. One UI 9 has matured into a genuinely good Android skin. The S Pen adds real utility. These are not revolutionary features individually, but together they create a device that does everything well.

Who should buy it? If you use your phone as your primary camera, if you need the S Pen for notes and documents, if you consume a lot of media, and if you want a device that will last seven years with updates, the S26 Ultra is the right choice.

Who should skip it? If you prefer a smaller phone, if you want the absolute best camera for low-light photography, or if you are tempted by the foldable form factor, you have better options.

As for me, I will keep using the S26 Ultra. It has earned its place in my pocket. And after six months, I no longer look at other phones with envy. That is the highest compliment I can give a device.

## Appendix: Camera Settings I Actually Use

For readers who want specifics, here are the camera settings I have found most reliable after extensive testing:

**Daylight photos:** 50MP mode with Scene Optimizer on. This gives the best balance of detail and file size. The 200MP mode is overkill for most daylight shots.

**Portrait mode:** 3x telephoto at f/2.4. This creates natural separation without the distortion of the main wide lens. Skin tones are slightly warmer than reality, which most people prefer.

**Low light:** Night mode with exposure set to 3 seconds. Handheld results are good. For best results, use a tripod or stabilize against a wall.

**Food photos:** 3x telephoto with food mode on. Samsung's food mode boosts saturation slightly, making dishes look more appetizing.

**Video:** 4K at 60fps with stabilization on. This is the sweet spot for quality and smoothness. 8K is reserved for short clips where you need the extra resolution.

**Selfies:** 12MP front camera with portrait mode. The front camera is not as good as the rear cameras — skin smoothing can be aggressive — but it is fine for social media.

I have a confession. I have bought four e-readers in the past eight years, hoping each time that the next one would finally be the device that replaces both my bookshelf and my stack of notebooks. The basic Kindle did well for reading. The Kobo handled library books better. The reMarkable 2 came close for writing. But every single device forced me to compromise on one side of the equation.

Then I got the Kindle Scribe 2 in January 2026, and six months later, I think I have finally found the one. But it is not perfect. And the things it gets wrong are frankly baffling for a device at this price point.

So here is my honest, six-month-later review of the Kindle Scribe 2, written entirely on the device itself.

## What the Kindle Scribe 2 Gets Right

Let me start with what works, because the list is genuinely long.

### The Screen Is Gorgeous

The 10.2-inch E Ink display on the Scribe 2 uses Carta 1300 technology, and it shows. The contrast is noticeably better than the first-generation Scribe. Text looks like ink on paper, not like a screen trying to imitate ink. The 300 PPI resolution means even small font sizes remain crisp, and the front light with warm temperature adjustment makes reading in bed comfortable without disturbing my partner.

But the real improvement is the refresh rate. The Scribe 2 supports 30 Hz refresh during note-taking, which means the pen feels significantly more responsive than the reMarkable 2. There is still a tiny lag if you scribble fast, but it no longer feels like writing through molasses. For someone who takes meeting notes at speed, this was the single biggest reason I stuck with the device past the return window.

### Battery Life That Changes Your Relationship with Charging

Amazon claims twelve weeks of battery life on a single charge. That is not marketing exaggeration. In six months, I have charged the Scribe 2 exactly three times. Twice because I was going on a long trip and wanted to be safe, once because the battery dipped below 20 percent after two months of heavy use that included about an hour of daily note-taking.

Compare this to my iPad Pro, which needs charging every day and a half. Compare it to the reMarkable 2, which lasted about two weeks. The Scribe 2 has fundamentally changed how I think about device power. I no longer carry a charger. I no longer check battery percentage before leaving the house. The device just works, for weeks on end, and that freedom is surprisingly liberating.

### Note-Taking: Good Enough to Replace My Paper Notebooks

I have been a Field Notes devotee for years. I love the smell of paper, the feel of a good pen gliding across a page, the satisfaction of filling a notebook and putting it on the shelf. But I also have eighteen half-filled notebooks on my shelf because I switched to a new one before finishing the old one, and I have lost countless meeting notes because I wrote them in the wrong notebook and could not find them later.

The Scribe 2 has solved this problem for me. The writing feel, with the Premium Pen that comes in the box, is the closest I have experienced to actual paper on a digital device. The textured screen protector helps tremendously. The pen has a rubber tip that provides just enough friction without feeling drag. After about a week of adjustment, I stopped thinking about the fact that I was writing on a screen.

The organizational system is basic but effective. Notebooks can be grouped into folders. Pages can be reordered. You can search handwritten notes, which is genuinely impressive — it converts my messy handwriting to text and finds keywords with surprising accuracy. Not perfect, but good enough that I have stopped manually tagging my notes.

I use the Scribe 2 for three distinct note-taking workflows. The first is daily journaling. Every morning I spend fifteen minutes writing stream-of-consciousness entries, and the ability to flip back through months of entries by searching for a specific date or keyword has been transformative. The second is meeting notes. I created a dedicated notebook for each recurring meeting, and I can review action items from three months ago without flipping through physical pages. The third is project planning. I sketch diagrams, draw mind maps, and outline documents directly on the device, then export them as PDFs when I need to share with collaborators.

### Reading Experience: Classic Kindle, Bigger Canvas

As an e-reader, the Scribe 2 is excellent. The larger screen means PDFs are actually readable without constant zooming and panicking. I read academic papers and work documents on it regularly, something I never did on my Paperwhite. The ability to annotate PDFs directly with the pen transforms the device into a research tool rather than just a consumption device.

Side-loading books via Send to Kindle works flawlessly. Library books via Libby integrate well. The Kindle store is what it is — overpriced for many titles, but convenient. The new Collections system in the 2026 software update finally allows nested collections, which made organizing my library of four hundred books actually manageable.

## Where It Falls Short

Now the part that frustrates me. The Scribe 2 makes choices that I simply cannot defend, and some of them feel like Amazon deliberately holding back features to protect other product lines.

### The Software Is Two Years Behind the Hardware

This is the biggest complaint. The hardware of the Scribe 2 is genuinely impressive, but the software feels like it was designed by a team that has never taken handwritten notes professionally.

There is no handwriting-to-text conversion that you can export as clean text. You can search your handwritten notes, but you cannot select a page of handwriting and convert it to a text document. This means that if I take meeting notes on the Scribe, I still have to manually transcribe them if I want to share them with my team. For a device positioned as a productivity tool, this omission is bizarre.

There is no real notebook cover system. Your notebooks appear as a list of titles with tiny thumbnails. There is no way to color-code them, add tags, or create a visual dashboard. The organizational system works for someone with ten notebooks. It fails for someone with forty.

There is no way to export notes in a useful format. The Scribe 2 can email notes as PDF, but the exported PDFs are not searchable. You cannot export as text, markdown, or anything actually useful. This feels like a deliberate limitation to avoid cannibalizing the reMarkable or Supernote markets, but it hurts users who want a genuinely productive device.

### The Premium Pen That Is Not Premium Enough

The included Premium Pen is good, but it is not great. The eraser on top works well, the shortcut button is convenient, but the pen uses AAAA batteries that are hard to find. When the battery died on a trip, I spent an afternoon searching stores before finding a single pack at an electronics shop.

The pen also lacks a magnet strong enough to stay attached to the device reliably. I have lost my pen inside my bag more times than I can count. The folio case holds it, but the folio adds bulk and cost. For a five-hundred-plus-dollar device, a proper magnetic attachment should be standard.

### PDF Annotation Is Clunky

While reading and annotating PDFs works, the experience is not smooth. Scrolling through a long PDF is slow. The device takes a noticeable second to render each new page. Annotations are not searchable. There is no split-screen view, so you cannot have notes on one side and the PDF on the other. For academic reading, the iPad with GoodNotes remains a better experience, even though the screen is worse for your eyes.

### Price

The Kindle Scribe 2 starts at $429 for the 16 GB model with Premium Pen. The 64 GB model is $489. That is expensive for an e-reader. It is positioned against the reMarkable 2 ($379 with Marker Plus) and the Supernote Nomad ($389). The Scribe 2 is the most expensive of the three, and while it offers the best reading experience, the note-taking software on the reMarkable is still superior.

### Build Quality and Design

The Scribe 2 is a well-built device. The aluminum back feels premium, and the bezels are wide enough to hold comfortably without triggering accidental touches. At 433 grams, it is lighter than an iPad Pro but heavier than a basic Kindle. I can hold it with one hand for about thirty minutes before my forearm gets tired, which is long enough for most reading sessions. The USB-C port on the bottom charges quickly with any standard charger, and the power button on the top is tactile and satisfying to press.

The device is IPX8 rated, meaning it can survive being dropped in water. I have not tested this personally, but knowing it can handle a splash at the pool or a spilled drink on my desk gives me peace of mind that I never had with paper notebooks.

### What About Competitors?

I mentioned the reMarkable 2 and Supernote Nomad earlier, and it is worth expanding on how they compare after living with all three.

The reMarkable 2 has superior note-taking software. The handwriting conversion works. The organization with tags and folders is more flexible. The writing feel, especially with the Marker Plus, is slightly better. But the reMarkable 2 has worse battery life, no front light (you cannot read in the dark), and no access to the Kindle ecosystem. It is a better notebook but a worse reader.

The Supernote Nomad has the best organizational system of the three, with keywords, titles, and a star system that makes finding notes effortless. It also supports more export formats. But the screen is smaller (7.8 inches), the device feels less premium, and the learning curve is steeper. It is a tool for power users who are willing to trade simplicity for capability.

The Scribe 2 sits in the middle. It is the best reader, the most polished hardware, and the easiest to use out of the box. But it is the worst at note-taking productivity features. Your choice depends on which axis matters more to you.

## Who Should Buy the Kindle Scribe 2

After six months, I have a clear picture of who this device is for.

**Buy it if:** You read a lot of Kindle books and want to take handwritten notes in the same device. You work with PDFs regularly and want to annotate them. You want one device that handles both reading and basic note-taking without carrying two gadgets. You value battery life above all else. You are already invested in the Kindle ecosystem.

**Skip it if:** Your primary need is professional-grade note-taking with robust organization and export. You need to share handwritten notes as text regularly. You want the best writing feel possible. You are on a budget. You do not read books on your e-reader and only want a digital notebook. In all these cases, a dedicated device will serve you better.

## The Verdict After Six Months

The Kindle Scribe 2 is the best e-reader I have ever used, and the most frustrating note-taking device I have owned. It excels at reading and basic note-taking but falls short on the productivity features that would make it truly indispensable.

I have not stopped using it. I use it daily. But I use it as a reader that I can also scribble on, not as the full notebook replacement I hoped for. The hardware is five stars. The software is three stars. The combination is a solid four-star device that comes frustratingly close to greatness.

If Amazon invests in the software over the next year, fixes the export limitations, and adds proper handwriting conversion, the Scribe 2 could be the ultimate device for knowledge workers. As of July 2026, it is the best option for someone who reads a lot and takes occasional notes, but not yet the paper replacement the world has been waiting for.

**Rating: 4/5** — Excellent hardware held back by software that has not caught up yet. Buy for the reading experience and basic note-taking. Wait for software updates if you need serious productivity features.

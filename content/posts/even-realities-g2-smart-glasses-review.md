## The Smart Glasses That Do One Thing Well

I have been wearing the Even Realities G2 for two weeks now. Not in a lab, not at a desk with a tester checklist — I wore them to coffee shops, on calls, on a motorcycle ride through Saigon traffic, and to a dinner where the person across from me had no idea I was getting live transcriptions of their Cantonese. That last part feels a little strange to admit, but it is also the point.

The G2 is not trying to replace your phone, your laptop, or your camera. It is not an AR headset for immersive gaming. It is not a pair of Ray-Ban Meta frames that films your morning commute for TikTok. The Even Realities G2 is the most focused smart glasses I have tested in 2026, and its focus is what makes it both brilliant and polarising.

No camera. No speakers. No full-colour display. Just a green monochrome heads-up display that hovers information at the top-right of your vision — closed captions for real life, turn-by-turn arrows, caller ID, and AI-generated meeting notes that appear without you pulling out a phone. It weighs 36 grams.

When I first put them on, my reaction was: *this is it?* A tiny green window showing me the time and a notification dot. No flashy demos, no spatial video. But after two weeks, I stopped noticing the glasses and started noticing that I was not reaching for my phone. That, I think, is the real test.

## Design and Build: Glasses First, Gadget Second

The G2 looks like a normal pair of eyewear. That sounds like a throwaway compliment until you remember that almost every other smart glasses product on the market in 2026 — the Meta Ray-Ban, the Xreal Air, the Google Glass Enterprise 3 — makes you look like you are either a tech demo or a surveillance contractor. The G2 does not.

Even Realities offers the G2 in two frame shapes — round and square — and three colour options (matte black, gunmetal, and tortoiseshell). The frame uses titanium temples and a magnesium front, materials chosen for weight rather than cost. At 36 grams, they are lighter than most prescription glasses I have owned. You forget you are wearing them after about twenty minutes.

The arms are slightly thicker than standard glasses to house the electronics, but not enough to draw attention. The charging contacts sit at the hinge, magnetically attaching to a USB-C cable or the included charging case. The case itself is a soft fabric pouch with a rigid insert — smaller than a glasses case, about the size of a passport. It holds seven additional full charges for the glasses.

IP67 rating means they survive rain and splashes. I rode through a brief Saigon downpour with them on and they worked fine. I would not take them swimming, but daily life is not a problem.

The only physical control is a touch-sensitive area on the right temple. Swipe forward to accept a call or dismiss a notification, swipe back to go back, tap to confirm. It works reliably about 85 percent of the time. The other 15 percent, you find yourself swiping twice or readjusting the fit. It is the weakest part of the hardware.

## The Display: Green, Small, and Brilliant

The G2 uses a dual-layer micro-LED waveguide display. That is a mouthful, so here is what it means practically: a small green monochrome image is projected into your right eye's peripheral vision at the top right. It looks like a floating notification badge about the size of a postage stamp held at arm's length.

Green only. No colour, no video, no animated content. This is deliberate — monochrome micro-LED draws minimal power and produces no glare in low light. The text is sharp enough to read easily even in bright outdoor sunlight. I tested it at noon in Saigon (approximately 100,000 lux) and could read transcription text without squinting.

The display sits far enough in your periphery that it does not block your view. You have to consciously look up and right to read it. This is the correct design choice for a productivity device: information when you want it, invisible when you do not. It never gave me motion sickness or eye strain, even after four hours of continuous wear during a work session.

What you cannot do is watch video, browse photos, or do anything visual. The G2 is text-only, and it is not trying to be anything else.

## The R1 Smart Ring: A Clever Companion

The R1 ring is optional at $249, and honestly, it should be included in the price. It pairs via Bluetooth and serves as both a health tracker and a remote control for the glasses.

The ring tracks steps, heart rate, sleep stages, and SpO2. Accuracy is decent — within 5 percent of my Apple Watch on step count, slightly worse on heart rate during exercise. Sleep tracking is surprisingly good; the ring caught my light and deep sleep phases consistently over two weeks.

As a controller, the ring lets you swipe and tap to interact with the glasses without touching your temple. This is useful when you are in a meeting and do not want to look like you are adjusting your glasses every thirty seconds. The haptic feedback on the ring is subtle but noticeable.

Battery life on the ring is about four days. It charges wirelessly on a small puck included in the box. The ring itself is ceramic-coated and looks like a sleek wedding band. I wore it alongside my usual watch and nobody asked about it.

## Real-World Features: What the G2 Actually Does

### Live Transcription

This is the killer app. The G2's Conversate system transcribes speech in over 30 languages in real time, displaying captions in the HUD. In practice: I sat through a 45-minute client call where the other party spoke mixed English and Cantonese. The G2 transcribed both into English text scrolling at the top of my vision. Accuracy was roughly 92 percent for English, 85 percent for Cantonese.

The latency is about half a second — fast enough to follow conversation, slow enough that you notice the delay if you stare at it. The trick is not to stare. Let the captions float in your periphery and glance only when you miss a word.

There is no recording. No transcript saved to the cloud. The text streams live and disappears. This makes it useful for real-time understanding without creating privacy issues.

### Navigation

Google Maps directions appear as turn-by-turn arrows in the HUD. This is surprisingly freeing. I walked across an unfamiliar neighbourhood in Ho Chi Minh City following arrow cues without once pulling out my phone. The GPS accuracy via the phone's connection is good enough for pedestrian use — within about five metres.

Cycling mode works similarly. I would not recommend it for motorcycle navigation at speed; the glance timing is too tight and the green text washes out slightly under direct sun at certain angles.

### Notifications

Caller ID, message previews, calendar alerts. You can configure which apps push to the glasses from the companion app on your phone. I set it to calls, messages, and calendar only. Everything else stays on my phone where it belongs.

Notification text scrolls once and fades. No persistent badges. This is deliberate — the G2 is designed to reduce phone pickup, not replace your lock screen. It worked for me. I picked up my phone about 40 percent less during the two-week test, purely because I could triage notifications on my face.

### Meeting Notes (AI)

This feature is in beta. You tap the ring to start recording a conversation, and the G2 transcribes and summarises it into notes on the companion app. The summaries are usable but generic — "discussed project timeline, agreed on next steps, action items assigned." It is not as good as a dedicated AI note-taker like Otter or Fireflies, but it frees you from typing during a meeting.

The catch: transcription processing happens on your phone, not on the glasses. The G2 has no onboard AI chip. It streams audio to the phone via Bluetooth, and the phone processes and sends text back. This means your phone battery takes a hit. I lost about 20 percent per hour during intensive transcription use.

### Teleprompter

A surprisingly useful mode. You paste text into the companion app, and it scrolls line by line on the HUD. I tested it for a five-minute recorded video script. Being able to speak directly into the camera while reading cue text in my periphery felt unnatural for the first thirty seconds, then became effortless. Public speakers will love this.

## Battery Life: The Real Differentiator

The G2 claims two days of battery life. In my testing, I got:

- About 10 hours of continuous use with moderate notification load (no transcription).
- About 6 hours with active transcription.
- About 8 hours with navigation.

The charging case adds seven more full charges. The glasses charge from empty to full in about 90 minutes. The case itself charges via USB-C and takes about two hours for a full cycle.

This is the category leader for battery life among smart glasses in 2026. The Meta Ray-Ban gets about 4-5 hours with intermittent recording. The Xreal Air needs to be plugged into a phone for power. The Google Glass Enterprise 3 manages about 8 hours. The G2 wins by a clear margin.

## Where It Falls Short

No product is perfect, and the G2 has real problems.

**The app ecosystem is thin.** There is no SDK for third-party developers. Everything the G2 does comes from Even Realities' own apps. A device like this needs a thriving app community to stay relevant beyond year one. The company says an SDK is coming, but it was not available during my review period.

**Bluetooth range is short.** The glasses need to stay within about five metres of your phone. Walk into another room and the connection stutters. Walk to the kitchen and the transcription drops. This is fine for desk work but limiting in a house or office.

**The touch controls are inconsistent.** I mentioned this earlier, but it deserves emphasis. The 85 percent reliability means you will miss a swipe at least once per session. When it happens mid-conversation, you look like you are having a seizure adjusting your glasses.

**No audio.** The G2 has no speakers and no microphone for calls. You still need earbuds or your phone speaker for phone calls. This limits the "glasses as all-day companion" vision — you are wearing two wearables instead of one.

**The price.** $599 for the glasses plus $249 for the R1 ring totals $848. That is iPhone SE territory. For a device that does three things well but nothing spectacularly, the value equation is tight.

## The Privacy Argument

The lack of a camera is not just a feature — it is the entire thesis of the G2. In 2026, public distrust of always-recording wearables is at an all-time high. Meta's Ray-Ban glasses have been banned from bars, gyms, and workplaces across multiple countries. Google Glass has never fully recovered from its "glasshole" reputation a decade ago.

Even Realities sidesteps this entirely. The G2 has nothing to record. No camera, no microphone that streams audio to the cloud (the mic is only used for live transcription, processed locally on your phone). You cannot take photos, you cannot record video, you cannot livestream.

This makes the G2 socially acceptable in ways that other smart glasses are not. I wore them through a full day of meetings, coffee shops, and street food stalls. Nobody asked if I was recording. Nobody looked at me twice. The only question I got was "are those new glasses?" from a colleague who noticed the slightly thicker arms.

For privacy-conscious professionals — and for anyone who works in environments where cameras are banned — the G2 is the only real option in 2026.

## Should You Buy It?

Buy the Even Realities G2 if:

- You need real-time transcription for multilingual meetings or conversations.
- You want navigation cues without pulling out your phone.
- You want to reduce phone distraction without going full dumbphone.
- You work in a camera-restricted environment but still want smart glasses features.
- You give presentations or record video scripts and want a teleprompter.

Skip the G2 if:

- You want to record photos or video hands-free.
- You want a full-colour AR experience with video playback.
- You want to take phone calls through your glasses.
- You are on a tight budget — $599 is steep for a notification display.

## Final Verdict

The Even Realities G2 is the first pair of smart glasses I would actually wear every day. Not because they are flashy or futuristic — they are deliberately neither — but because they solve a real problem: getting useful information into your field of vision without demanding your attention or compromising your privacy.

The green display is small. The features are limited. The touch controls are finicky. All of those are true. But the core loop — glance, process, return to the real world — works so well that I found myself reaching for them every morning alongside my keys and phone.

In a market full of devices trying to do everything, the G2 does one thing well: it puts information where your eyes already are, without adding drama.

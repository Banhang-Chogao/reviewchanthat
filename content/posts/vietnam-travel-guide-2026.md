I have traveled to Vietnam seven times in the past four years, and every single trip taught me something new. The first time I landed in Hanoi, I had no idea that crossing the street would be the most terrifying two minutes of my life. The second time, I learned that the $2 bowl of pho from a street cart in Saigon tastes better than any restaurant version I have ever had. By the seventh trip, I stopped making plans and started letting the country surprise me.

This guide is for first-time visitors who want the real Vietnam — not the packaged tour version, not the backpacker bubble, but the actual places, prices, and experiences that make this country unforgettable. I am writing this in July 2026, so all prices and practical details are current as of this summer.

## When to Visit Vietnam

Vietnam is a long, skinny country stretching over 1,600 kilometers, which means the weather varies dramatically from north to south. There is no single "best time" — it depends on where you want to go.

**October to April** is the dry season in the south and the cooler season in the north. This is peak tourist season. Ha Long Bay and Hanoi are pleasant but crowded. Ho Chi Minh City is at its best — warm days, low humidity, no rain.

**May to September** is the rainy season in the south and summer in the north. The north gets hot and humid, with occasional heavy rain. The south sees daily afternoon downpours that last an hour and then clear up. This is low season, which means cheaper flights and emptier attractions. I personally love traveling in June and July because the landscapes are at their greenest.

If I had to pick one ideal window for a first-timer covering both north and south, I would choose **March-April or October-November**. The weather is mild everywhere, crowds are manageable, and prices are reasonable.

## How Much Does Vietnam Cost in 2026?

Let me give you real numbers based on my most recent trip in April 2026.

**Budget traveler (dorm beds, street food, local buses):** $25-35 per day
**Mid-range traveler (private rooms, nice restaurants, occasional taxis):** $50-80 per day
**Luxury traveler (resorts, fine dining, private drivers):** $150-250 per day

A bowl of pho or bun cha costs $1.50-3 at a street stall. A banh mi is $1-2. A beer at a local bar is $0.50-1. A cappuccino at a specialty coffee shop is $2.50-4.

Accommodation is where your biggest savings come from. A decent private room with air conditioning in a mid-range hotel costs $25-40 per night outside of major tourist areas. In Hanoi's Old Quarter or Saigon's District 1, expect $35-60. A dorm bed in a hostel costs $8-15.

Domestic flights between major cities cost $30-60 one way if booked a few weeks in advance. The Hanoi-Da Nang and Ho Chi Minh-Da Nang routes are served by multiple airlines keeping prices competitive.

## Visa Requirements 2026

As of July 2026, citizens from 25 countries including the UK, Germany, France, Italy, Spain, and Russia can enter Vietnam visa-free for up to 45 days. Citizens of other countries need an e-visa, which costs $25 and takes three working days to process. The e-visa allows a single entry for up to 90 days.

Apply at the official Vietnam e-visa portal. Do not use third-party websites that charge inflated fees. I have seen friends pay $80 for a service that costs $25 if you do it yourself.

## 7-Day Northern Vietnam Itinerary

This is the itinerary I recommend for first-timers with limited time. It covers the highlights without feeling rushed.

**Day 1: Hanoi arrival.** Settle in, take a walk around Hoan Kiem Lake, and eat bun cha at the famous Huong Lien restaurant where Obama had dinner with Anthony Bourdain. The original Obama combo costs $3.50.

**Day 2: Hanoi Old Quarter.** Spend the day wandering the 36 streets of the Old Quarter, each named after the guild that historically sold goods there. Visit the Temple of Literature, Vietnam's first university, and eat egg coffee at Cafe Giang. The coffee is $1.50 and life-changing.

**Day 3: Ha Long Bay cruise.** Take a day cruise or overnight junk boat through the limestone karsts. An overnight cruise with meals and kayaking costs $80-150 per person. Book through your hotel or a reputable agency in Hanoi. Do not book on the street — scams are common.

**Day 4: Ninh Binh.** Take a two-hour bus from Hanoi to Ninh Binh, often called "Ha Long Bay on land." Rent a bicycle and ride through the rice fields to Tam Coc, take a boat ride through the caves, and climb Hang Mua for the panoramic view. The entire day costs under $30.

**Day 5: Sapa (optional).** If you have extra time, take the overnight train from Hanoi to Lao Cai and then a bus to Sapa. The rice terraces are breathtaking from September to November. The train costs $25-40 for a soft sleeper.

**Day 6: Return to Hanoi.** Fly to your next destination or head south.

## 14-Day North to South Itinerary

For travelers with two weeks, start in Hanoi and work your way south.

**Days 1-3: Hanoi + Ha Long Bay** (same as above)
**Days 4-5: Ninh Binh + overnight train to Hue**
**Days 6-7: Hue.** Visit the Imperial Citadel, take a boat on the Perfume River, and eat bun bo Hue — the best beef noodle soup in Vietnam. A bowl costs $2.
**Days 8-9: Da Nang + Hoi An.** Ride the cable car up Ba Na Hills, relax on My Khe Beach, and spend an evening in Hoi An's lantern-lit Ancient Town. The tailor shops in Hoi An can make a custom suit or dress in 24 hours for $80-150.
**Days 10-11: Ho Chi Minh City.** Visit the War Remnants Museum, explore the Cu Chi Tunnels, and eat your way through District 1. The museum costs $1.50 and is one of the most impactful experiences in the country.
**Days 12-13: Mekong Delta.** Take a day trip to the floating markets of Cai Rang and the coconut candy workshops in Ben Tre. A guided day tour costs $30-50.
**Day 14: Depart from Ho Chi Minh City.**

## Getting Around

**Domestic flights** are the fastest way to cover long distances. Vietjet, Bamboo Airways, and Vietnam Airlines all fly the major routes. I prefer Bamboo for the legroom and included luggage.

**Trains** are slow but scenic. The Reunification Express runs from Hanoi to Ho Chi Minh City, taking about 30 hours. A soft sleeper berth costs $35-50. The overnight train from Hanoi to Sapa or Hue is a memorable experience.

**Buses** are cheap and connect every city. VIP sleeper buses with reclining seats cost $10-20 for long distances. The roads can be rough, so bring motion sickness medicine.

**Taxis and ride-hailing.** Grab is the dominant app and works in every major city. A typical ride within the city costs $2-5. Avoid hailing taxis on the street — the meter is often rigged. Always use Grab.

## Food Safety and Eating Well

The number one fear I hear from first-time visitors is food poisoning. Here is the truth: street food in Vietnam is generally safe, but you need to be smart about it.

Eat where locals eat. A stall packed with Vietnamese customers at lunchtime is almost certainly safe. Avoid places where the food has been sitting out for hours. Watch how the vendor handles the ingredients — if they use gloves and keep things refrigerated, you are fine.

My personal rule: I eat street food every day in Vietnam and have gotten sick exactly once in seven trips. That one time was from a fancy restaurant in a tourist area, not from a street cart.

Drink only bottled or boiled water. Ice in Vietnam is made from purified water and is safe — I have never had an issue with it.

## Cultural Tips That Actually Matter

**Crossing the street:** Do not make eye contact with drivers. Do not run. Do not stop. Walk at a steady pace and the traffic will flow around you. It feels wrong but it works.

**Temple etiquette:** Cover your shoulders and knees. Remove your shoes before entering. Do not point your feet at Buddha statues. Women should not touch monks.

**Bargaining:** Expected in markets, but do it respectfully. Offer 60-70 percent of the asking price and settle around 80 percent. If the price is already fair, do not bargain.

**Tipping:** Not expected but appreciated. Round up the taxi fare or leave 5-10 percent at nice restaurants.

## What Nobody Tells You

Vietnam is not a difficult country to travel in, but it is different. The chaos that seems overwhelming on day one becomes charming by day five. The constant beeping of motorbike horns stops being noise and becomes a rhythm. The woman squatting on the sidewalk making the best banh mi of your life becomes a normal part of your morning.

The thing I love most about Vietnam is that it does not try to be easy. It rewards patience, curiosity, and a sense of humor. If you arrive with an open mind and a flexible itinerary, you will leave wondering why you did not come sooner.

The Vietnam I experienced on my seventh trip was completely different from the first one. The country changes fast — new highways, modern airports, a cafe culture that rivals Melbourne. But the core stays the same: generous people, incredible food, and landscapes that make you forget your phone exists.

Pack light, bring an appetite, and book that flight. Vietnam in 2026 is ready for you.
